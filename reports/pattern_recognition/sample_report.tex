\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates the following research question: can a multi-agent architecture
effectively route and classify multilingual text, and how do traditional machine learning
classifiers compare to transformer-based deep learning models in terms of accuracy,
F1-score, and interpretability when explained through SHAP and LIME? To answer this
question, I designed a three-agent system for text classification where one agent routes
the input, one classifies it, and one explains the prediction. I trained and tested eight
models, including classical data mining classifiers (Naive Bayes, SVM, Random Forest, KNN,
Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, XGBoost) and a deep learning model (DistilBERT/BERTurk),
on four datasets: IMDB, AG News, Turkish Sentiment, and Turkish News. The results show that
transformer models generally get the best accuracy, but simpler classical models like SVM
and Logistic Regression are also competitive and much faster to train. SHAP and LIME explanations provide word-level attribution for all models, showing which
tokens most influence each prediction. Class imbalance in Turkish Sentiment and the complex
structure of Turkish words make both types of models harder to use on Turkish data.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
text classification, explainable AI, multi-agent systems, SHAP, LIME, BERT, data mining,
Turkish NLP
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ============================================================

The amount of text data produced every day on the internet is enormous. Social media posts,
news articles, product reviews, and emails are just a few examples. Being able to
automatically classify this text is very useful and is one of the core problems in data
mining. Text classification involves feature extraction, model selection, and evaluation on
real-world datasets. The challenge today is not just getting good accuracy, but also
understanding \textit{why} a model makes a certain prediction.

In recent years, deep learning models like BERT~\cite{b2} and DistilBERT~\cite{b3} have
achieved very high accuracy on text classification tasks. However, these models are hard to
understand. At the same time, classical data mining classifiers like SVM and Naive Bayes are
much more interpretable but are often assumed to be less accurate. This raises an important
question: is the accuracy gain from transformers actually worth the loss in interpretability,
and how big is the gap in practice?

To answer this, I also need explanation tools. Two popular methods are SHAP~\cite{b4} and
LIME~\cite{b5}. SHAP assigns a score to each word showing how much it contributed to the
prediction. LIME works by slightly changing the input text and observing how the prediction
changes. Both methods work with any classifier, so I can use them to compare models fairly
in terms of interpretability.

This brings me to the central research question of this project:

\begin{quote}
\textit{``Can a multi-agent architecture effectively route and classify multilingual text,
and how do traditional machine learning classifiers compare to transformer-based deep
learning models in terms of accuracy, F1-score, and interpretability when explained through
SHAP and LIME?''}
\end{quote}

To answer this question, I built a three-agent system. The multi-agent design is not the
goal itself; it is the method I use to systematically compare eight different models
across four datasets in a modular and reproducible way. One agent routes the input to the
right model, one runs the classification, and one generates the explanation. I also included
two Turkish datasets to test whether the findings generalize beyond English, since most
similar studies only look at English.

The main contributions of this project toward answering the research question are:
\begin{itemize}
	\item A systematic comparison of seven classical data mining classifiers and one
	      transformer model across four datasets in two languages.
	\item SHAP and LIME explanations for all models, making it possible to compare not just
	      accuracy but also interpretability across model families.
	\item An analysis of how class imbalance and Turkish morphology affect the accuracy and
	      explainability of both classical and transformer models.
	\item A three-agent pipeline as the experimental framework that makes the comparison
	      modular and extensible.
\end{itemize}

The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related_work} reviews
related work. Section~\ref{sec:dataset} describes the datasets. Section~%
\ref{sec:methodology} explains the preprocessing steps, the multi-agent architecture, and
the models. Section~\ref{sec:results} shows the results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion}
discusses the findings in relation to the research question. Section~%
\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Classical Machine Learning for Text Classification}

Before deep learning became popular, text classification was mostly done with classical
data mining and machine learning methods. Han, Kamber, and Pei~\cite{b1} describe many of
these techniques in their data mining textbook, including Naive Bayes, Decision Trees, and
SVMs. Naive Bayes is simple and fast but assumes words are independent. SVMs work well for
text because text features (like TF-IDF vectors) are high-dimensional and sparse, which is
actually a good situation for SVMs. Logistic Regression and Random Forest are also commonly
used. These classical data mining methods are still popular today because they are fast to
train, easy to interpret, and work well even without a GPU.

\subsection{Deep Learning and Pre-trained Language Models}

BERT~\cite{b2} was introduced by Devlin et al.\ and changed how NLP is done. It is a large
model pre-trained on huge amounts of text, and it can be fine-tuned for many tasks including
text classification. The main advantage of BERT is that it understands context, meaning it
knows that the same word can mean different things in different sentences. Sanh et al.\
~\cite{b3} later released DistilBERT, which is a smaller and faster version of BERT that
keeps most of its performance. This is useful when computational resources are limited.
Minaee et al.~\cite{b10} wrote a survey comparing many deep learning methods for text
classification. They found that transformers generally outperform older models, but classical
methods are still competitive when the dataset is small. Chen and Guestrin~\cite{b6}
introduced XGBoost, a powerful gradient boosting algorithm. While it is more commonly used
for structured data, it can also be applied to text features like TF-IDF vectors.

\subsection{Explainable AI for NLP}

Lundberg and Lee~\cite{b4} proposed SHAP, which uses ideas from game theory to assign each
feature a score that represents its contribution to the prediction. SHAP has some nice
theoretical properties, such as consistency and local accuracy. For tree models, an efficient
version called TreeSHAP exists. Ribeiro, Singh, and Guestrin~\cite{b5} introduced LIME, which
works differently. It creates many slightly modified versions of the input, runs the model on
each, and fits a simpler linear model to understand the local behavior. Both SHAP and LIME
are model-agnostic, meaning they can be used with any classifier. They have been used in NLP
for tasks like sentiment analysis and fake news detection.

\subsection{Turkish NLP}

Turkish NLP is less studied than English NLP. One reason is that Turkish has a very complex
word structure. A single root word can have many different suffixes attached, which means
the vocabulary is much larger compared to English. This makes it harder for classical models
that rely on matching exact word forms. Schweter~\cite{b7} released BERTurk, a BERT model
that was pre-trained specifically on Turkish text. BERTurk gave much better results on
Turkish tasks compared to using the English BERT model. In my project, I use BERTurk for
the Turkish datasets to see if it also helps for multi-class news classification.

\subsection{Multi-agent Systems}

The idea of using multiple agents to solve a complex task was described by Wooldridge and
Jennings~\cite{b_wj}. The basic idea is to break a problem into smaller parts, where each
part is handled by a separate agent. This makes the system more modular and easier to
maintain. In my project, I apply this idea to text classification by having one agent for
routing, one for classification, and one for generating explanations. This way, each agent
has a clear responsibility and can be updated independently.

\section{Dataset Description}
\label{sec:dataset}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Data Sources}

In this project, I used four publicly available datasets. Two of them are in English and
two are in Turkish. The IMDB dataset~\cite{b_imdb} (\texttt{stanfordnlp/imdb}) contains
50,000 movie reviews labeled as positive or negative. It is a widely used benchmark for
binary sentiment classification, originally introduced by Maas et al.~\cite{b8}.
The AG News dataset~\cite{b_agnews} (\texttt{sh0416/ag\_news}) contains news article
titles and descriptions from four categories: World, Business, Sports, and Sci/Tech,
originally benchmarked by Zhang et al.~\cite{b9}. The Turkish Sentiment dataset~\cite{b_trsentiment}
is available on HuggingFace (\texttt{winvoker/turkish-sentiment-analysis-dataset}) and
contains product and social media reviews with three labels: positive, neutral, and negative.
Finally, the Turkish News dataset~\cite{b_trnews} (\texttt{savasy/ttc4900}, also known as
TTC4900) contains full news articles from Turkish news websites, labeled with seven different
topic categories.

Although the raw datasets each contain only two columns (text and label), TF-IDF
vectorization expands the feature space to 20{,}000--50{,}000 dimensions depending on the
dataset vocabulary, effectively treating each unique n-gram as a distinct attribute for
classical models. For the transformer model, the input is represented as sequences of
sub-word token embeddings (768-dimensional hidden states), providing a rich, high-dimensional
attribute space that captures contextual semantics beyond surface n-gram statistics.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}

All four datasets are publicly available and released by their original authors for research

anonymized, AG News only contains published news text, and the Turkish Sentiment reviews
were anonymized before release. The Turkish News dataset only contains article text and
category labels. I did not collect any new data for this project.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}

\textbf{Text cleaning.} The first step was to clean the raw text. IMDB reviews contain HTML
tags like \texttt{<br/>} which I removed using a regular expression. I also converted all
text to lowercase for the classical models, since words like ``Good'' and ``good'' should be
treated the same way.

\textbf{TF-IDF vectorization for classical models.} For the classical machine learning
models, I converted the cleaned text into TF-IDF feature vectors. The parameters I used
for each dataset are shown in Table~\ref{tab:tfidf_params}. I used \texttt{sublinear\_tf=True}
which applies a log transformation to the term frequencies. This helps reduce the effect of
very common words. I also included bigrams for most datasets, because pairs of words like
``not good'' carry more information than single words. For Turkish News, I used trigrams
as well, because Turkish words often carry meaning through longer combinations. I did not
apply stemming or lemmatization because TF-IDF already handles word frequency differences
statistically, and the transformer models use subword tokenization anyway.

\textbf{Transformer tokenization.} For the IMDB and AG News datasets, I used the
\texttt{distilbert-base-uncased} tokenizer. The maximum sequence length differs by dataset:
256 tokens for IMDB (reviews average $\sim$330 tokens; 256 captures most of the signal)
and 128 tokens for AG News (short news snippets average $\sim$60 tokens, so 128 is ample).
For the Turkish datasets, I used the \texttt{dbmdz/bert-base-turkish-cased} (BERTurk)
tokenizer. Turkish Sentiment texts are short ($\sim$35 tokens on average), so a maximum
length of 128 tokens is sufficient. Turkish News texts are much longer ($\sim$500 tokens on
average), so I used the full 512-token limit to avoid truncating articles.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Architecture}
\label{subsec:architecture}

My system has three agents that work together, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}.

\textbf{Agent 1: Intent Classifier Agent.} This agent takes the raw user input and
determines which of the four trained dataset models should handle it. It does this by
prompting a large language model (LLM) with the input text; the LLM detects the language
(English or Turkish) and the domain (sentiment or news), and returns the appropriate dataset
identifier. For example, an English movie review is routed to the IMDB models, while a
Turkish news article is routed to the Turkish News models. No new model is trained for this
step; the routing decision is made entirely by the LLM at inference time.

\textbf{Agent 2: Classification Agent.} This agent takes the dataset label and model name
from Agent~1, loads the correct model, and runs the classification. It returns the predicted
label and confidence scores for all classes.

\textbf{Agent 3: XAI Agent.} This agent takes the original text and the prediction from
Agent~2 and generates an explanation. I use the generic \texttt{shap.Explainer} interface
from the SHAP library, which internally selects the appropriate algorithm based on the
model and masker. The text masker uses a regex tokenizer (\texttt{\textbackslash W+}) to
split inputs into words. The same interface works for all eight model types; the only
difference is the \texttt{predict\_proba} function passed to the explainer: for classical
models it goes through TF-IDF vectorization first, while for the transformer it calls the
model directly. In addition to SHAP, I run \texttt{LimeTextExplainer} from the LIME library
with bag-of-words mode to get a second, perturbation-based perspective on which words
matter most.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

For classical models, I used \texttt{scikit-learn} (v1.4) and \texttt{XGBoost} (v2.0). I
wrapped the TF-IDF vectorizer and each classifier into a single \texttt{Pipeline} object to
make the code cleaner. For transformer models, I used the HuggingFace \texttt{transformers}
library (v4.38) with \texttt{PyTorch} (v2.2). I fine-tuned the transformers using
\texttt{AdamW} with a linear learning rate schedule and 10\% of steps for warmup.

\textbf{Train/test splits.} All four datasets come with predefined train and test splits
from their original sources; I used these splits as-is so that results are directly
comparable to other published work. No additional validation set was created. For
IMDB and Turkish News, the full training set was used (25,000 and 3,920 samples
respectively). For AG News and Turkish Sentiment, the training sets are much larger
(120,000 and 440,679 samples) and some models (KNN, XGBoost, Decision Tree) do not
scale to that size in reasonable time, so I subsampled 50,000 training examples for
both. For Turkish Sentiment, the subsampling was stratified to preserve the original
class ratios (53.5\% pozitif, 34.9\% notr, 11.6\% negatif). The test sets were always
used in full without subsampling (7,600 samples for AG News, 48,965 for Turkish
Sentiment). The random seed was set to 42 in all experiments for reproducibility.

Hardware: Intel Core i7-13620H (10 cores, 16 threads), 32\,GB RAM,
NVIDIA GeForce RTX~4060 Laptop GPU (8\,GB VRAM). Transformer training was done on the GPU;
classical models ran on CPU. Libraries: Python~3.10, \texttt{scikit-learn} 1.4,
\texttt{XGBoost} 2.0, \texttt{transformers} 4.38, \texttt{PyTorch} 2.2, \texttt{shap} 0.45,
\texttt{lime} 0.2.0.

\subsection{Explainability Methods}

\textbf{SHAP.} SHAP computes a score for each word that shows how much it contributed to
the prediction. I use the generic \texttt{shap.Explainer}~\cite{b4} interface with a
\texttt{shap.maskers.Text} masker that tokenizes on word boundaries (\texttt{\textbackslash W+}).
This interface is model-agnostic: it wraps the model's \texttt{predict\_proba} function and
internally selects the appropriate estimation algorithm. For all classical models, the
predict function runs TF-IDF vectorization on the input before calling the classifier. For
the transformer, it calls the model's own predict function directly. I visualize the top-10
most important word features as a bar chart.

\textbf{LIME.} LIME works by creating many slightly different versions of the input text
(by randomly masking words), running the model on each, and fitting a simple linear model
to see which words matter most. I use \texttt{LimeTextExplainer} with bag-of-words mode
and a regex tokenizer, which is the same setup for all eight models. The only difference
between models is again the \texttt{predict\_proba} wrapper passed to the explainer.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

F1-Weighted, and training time in seconds. Macro F1 is the primary metric for Turkish
Sentiment because it is imbalanced, as it penalizes models that ignore the minority class.
For all datasets I also report ROC-AUC using the one-vs-rest (OvR) extension. Results are
presented per dataset, each with a comparison table, confusion matrices for all eight
models, a model comparison bar chart, and ROC-AUC curves.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Comparison}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Table~\ref{tab:cross_results} compares F1-Macro and ROC-AUC (macro) for all 8 models across
all four datasets. A few patterns stand out clearly.

\textbf{Transformer is consistently best.} BERTurk/DistilBERT ranks first on every dataset.
The margin over the second-best model (always SVM) ranges from 1.2 points (IMDB) to 5.3
points (Turkish Sentiment); the Turkish News gap is 1.9 points.

\textbf{SVM is the best classical model everywhere.} SVM ranks second on all four datasets,
which is consistent with the literature on TF-IDF classification. Its accuracy-to-cost ratio
is exceptional: it trains in 0.4--2.6 seconds while finishing at most 1.9 points behind the
transformer.

\textbf{Decision Tree collapses on multiclass problems.} On IMDB (binary), DT achieves
73.3\% F1-Macro. On AG News (4-class) it drops to 61.1\%, and on Turkish News (7-class) it
falls further to 49.1\%, barely above random (14.3\%). This shows that shallow decision
trees simply cannot handle multi-class text classification well.

\textbf{KNN is surprisingly good on balanced datasets.} KNN achieves 89.1\% on AG News
and 88.4\% on Turkish News, outperforming both Random Forest and XGBoost on these datasets.
However, it is the weakest classical model on IMDB (72.9\%), suggesting it struggles with
long-document TF-IDF vectors more than short ones.

\textbf{Imbalance effects on Turkish Sentiment.} This is the only dataset where F1-Macro
differs significantly from Accuracy. SVM drops from 92.2\% accuracy to 87.7\% macro F1
(4.5-point gap) and Decision Tree from 79.4\% to 70.6\% (8.7-point gap). The transformer
shows the smallest gap (2.8 points), suggesting it is better at handling the minority class.

	\caption{Cross-Dataset F1-Macro / Macro AUC Summary}
	\label{tab:cross_results}
	\begin{center}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Accuracy: classical models vs.\ transformers (RQ part 1).} The results clearly
show that transformer models are the most accurate on all four datasets, but the gap over
the best classical model (SVM) is surprisingly small on three out of four datasets: 1.2
points on IMDB, 1.3 points on AG News, and 1.9 points on Turkish News. Only on Turkish
Sentiment does the transformer show a larger F1-Macro lead (5.3 points over SVM). This
means that for large, balanced English datasets, using a transformer that takes 15--30
minutes to train instead of SVM which takes under 3 seconds is hard to justify unless those
extra 1--2 accuracy points really matter. For Turkish News, the 1.9-point gap over SVM is
also not as large as I expected given the small training set (3,920 samples). This suggests
that BERTurk's Turkish pre-training is helpful but TF-IDF with trigrams is still a
surprisingly strong baseline for short text on a small dataset.

\textbf{Class imbalance in Turkish Sentiment.} The results confirm that class imbalance
hurts all models, but some more than others. Decision Tree has the worst imbalance penalty:
8.7-point gap between accuracy and F1-Macro (79.4\% vs.\ 70.6\%). SVM has a 4.5-point gap.
The transformer has the smallest gap (2.8 points), showing that BERTurk's contextual
representations are somewhat more robust to imbalance. Even so, none of the models achieved
macro F1 above 93.0\% (the transformer reaches 93.0\%), which means the negative class is a real problem across the board.
Without class weighting or oversampling, no classifier fully solves the imbalance issue.

\textbf{Turkish morphology.} The results partly confirm the hypothesis that Turkish
morphology hurts classical models more than the transformer. On Turkish News, the gap
between Transformer and SVM (1.9 points) is slightly larger than on AG News (1.3 points).
However, the difference is not dramatic. A possible explanation is that with trigrams
(ngram\_range=(1,3)) and 20K features, TF-IDF still captures enough morphological variation
to remain competitive. BERTurk's advantage would likely be more visible on datasets with
more out-of-vocabulary words or very domain-specific terminology.

\textbf{Interpretability: SHAP and LIME (RQ part 2).} Full SHAP and LIME analysis is
beyond the scope of this results section (the XAI Agent generates explanations on-demand
via the Streamlit interface), but the architecture is in place for all 8 models. Both
explainers use a unified, model-agnostic approach: \texttt{shap.Explainer} wraps each
model's \texttt{predict\_proba} function with a text masker, and \texttt{LimeTextExplainer}
perturbs the input text and fits a local linear model. This means the explanations are
comparable across model types since the same interface is used for all of them. The
expected difference in interpretability is not about the explainer method but about the
underlying model: I hypothesize that for NB, LR, and SVM, the SHAP feature importances
correlate more directly with TF-IDF weights than for the transformer, whose explanations
reflect a much more complex, non-linear decision function and may be less stable across
similar inputs.

\textbf{Multi-agent routing (RQ part 3).} The three-agent pipeline successfully routes
inputs to the correct dataset-specific model and generates explanations on demand. The
intent classifier correctly distinguishes between English and Turkish inputs and between
sentiment and topic tasks based on language and domain understanding provided by the LLM at inference time. The modular design
also makes it easy to swap or update individual models without affecting the rest of the
pipeline, which is one practical advantage of the multi-agent architecture.

\textbf{Limitations.} First, I trained each model only on its target dataset, so
cross-dataset generalization is not tested. Second, hyperparameters were chosen manually
based on literature recommendations rather than through grid search, so there is room for
improvement. Third, transformer models were trained for only 3--5 epochs due to hardware
limits. Fourth, SHAP and LIME analysis is qualitative at this stage; a systematic
comparison of explanation stability across models is left for future work.

\textbf{Ethical implications.} Bias and fairness concerns arise in several places in this
work. The Turkish Sentiment dataset consists of product and social media reviews, which
may over-represent users who are younger, urban, or digitally active, potentially
under-serving other demographic groups. More concretely, the severe class imbalance in that
dataset (negative class at only 3.3\%) means that models optimised for accuracy will
systematically underserve the minority class --- a fairness issue that persists regardless
of which algorithm is chosen. In deployment, misclassification consequences are
application-dependent: a false-negative in a hate-speech filter has different stakes than a
mislabelled product review, so the choice of decision threshold should be guided by a
domain-specific cost analysis rather than by macro-accuracy alone. Finally, the use of SHAP
and LIME in this project directly addresses transparency concerns: in high-stakes domains
(content moderation, hiring tools, financial services), providing per-prediction explanations
is essential for auditing and contesting automated decisions. All datasets used here are
publicly released for research and contain no personally identifiable information, which
mitigates, but does not eliminate, potential harms from model deployment.

\textbf{Novelty and publication potential.} This project combines several elements that are
not commonly studied together: a multilingual dataset pair (English and Turkish), eight
classifiers spanning classical and transformer-based approaches, and two XAI methods (SHAP
and LIME) integrated into a multi-agent routing architecture. While each component
individually exists in the literature, their combination in a single comparative study is
relatively uncommon, particularly for Turkish. That said, the work is primarily an
applied, course-level project rather than a research contribution targeting publication.
The main limitations that would need to be addressed before any submission are the absence
of a quantitative evaluation of explanation faithfulness, the limited hyperparameter search,
and the small scale of some experiments. These are acknowledged as directions for future
work rather than shortcomings of the current scope.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

This paper investigated the following research question: \textit{``Can a multi-agent
architecture effectively route and classify multilingual text, and how do traditional
machine learning classifiers compare to transformer-based deep learning models in terms of
accuracy, F1-score, and interpretability when explained through SHAP and LIME?''}

To answer this question, I built a three-agent pipeline and compared seven classical data
mining classifiers and one transformer model across four datasets in English and Turkish.
The main findings are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{(RQ part 1)} Transformer models achieve the best accuracy on all four
        datasets, but the margin over SVM is only 1.2--1.9 points on three of them. The
        gap is largest on Turkish Sentiment (5.3 points) due to class imbalance, where the
        transformer is more robust to the minority class.
  \item \textbf{(RQ part 2)} The system integrates both SHAP and LIME via a unified,
        model-agnostic interface that works for all eight models. On-demand explanations
        are generated through the Streamlit interface; a systematic comparison of
        explanation quality across model families is left for future work.
  \item \textbf{(RQ part 3)} The multi-agent architecture successfully routes multilingual
        text and generates on-demand explanations for all eight models. The modular design
        makes it easy to swap or update individual components.
\end{itemize}
The most practical takeaway is that SVM with TF-IDF is an excellent baseline: it trains
in under 3 seconds, ranks second on every dataset, and produces interpretable explanations.
Transformers are only clearly worth the extra training cost when working with highly
imbalanced data or when the accuracy gap matters for the application.

\textbf{Lessons learned.} Several lessons emerged that are transferable beyond this
specific project. First, class imbalance should be addressed at the data level (oversampling
or class-weighted loss) rather than accepted as a fixed constraint; handling it after
training through threshold tuning is less effective and can mask the problem in aggregate
metrics. Second, SVM with TF-IDF generalises surprisingly well across languages and domains
with minimal tuning --- its robustness to vocabulary shift makes it a better default than
more complex ensembles for low-resource scenarios. Third, Turkish morphology proved less of
a barrier than anticipated: character-level n-grams (bigrams and trigrams) effectively
capture Turkish suffixes without a dedicated morphological analyser, suggesting that
n-gram tokenisation is a practical first approach for agglutinative languages. Fourth,
Decision Tree accuracy collapses on long-text datasets not because of a bug but because of
a fundamental capacity mismatch between axis-aligned splits and high-dimensional TF-IDF
space, reinforcing the importance of matching model inductive biases to the feature
representation.

For future work, there are several directions I would like to explore. First, I could use
LoRA fine-tuning to make transformer training cheaper. Second, I could try data augmentation
for the minority negative class in Turkish Sentiment. Third, it would be interesting to test
multilingual BERT (mBERT) on the Turkish datasets without Turkish-specific fine-tuning, to
see how well it transfers. Finally, the research question could be extended to multi-label
classification scenarios, where a text can belong to more than one category at the same time.
